Decoding Cat Food Labels: What's Really Inside?

Choosing the right food for your feline friend can be overwhelming. Marketing claims like "natural" or "premium" don't tell the whole story. Let's learn how to read the actual ingredient list.

The Importance of Named Proteins

Cats are obligate carnivores, meaning they require meat to survive. The first ingredient should always be a named, high-quality protein source like "Chicken," "Turkey," or "Salmon." Avoid vague terms like "Meat by-products" or "Poultry meal" as the primary ingredient.

Watch Out for Hidden Carbs

Many commercial cat foods use cheap fillers to bulk up the product. Ingredients like corn, wheat, and soy are difficult for cats to digest and offer little nutritional value. Look for foods that use sweet potatoes or peas in moderation instead.

Understanding the "Guaranteed Analysis"

This section tells you the minimum percentages of crude protein and fat, and the maximum percentages of fiber and moisture. For adult cats, look for a minimum of 26% protein and 9% fat (dry matter basis).

Wet vs. Dry Food

Wet food is generally closer to a cat's natural diet and provides essential moisture, which is vital for kidney and urinary tract health. However, dry food is convenient and good for dental health. A mix of both is often the best approach.
